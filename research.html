Detecting Smurfing:
Competitive Integrity Through Behavioral Analytics

This project explores whether in game behavioral data can identify smurfing in Rocket League. By analyzing player satistics across rank tiers in Rocket League, we build behavioral profiles that reveal skill level regardless of account rank. Our goal is to detect experienced players on new accounts before traditional ranking systems can catch up.

Abstract

In competitive gaming, the practice of highly skilled players choosing to play on lower-ranked accounts is known as smurfing. This study proposes a data-driven approach for smurf detection using a combination of behavioral profiling and machine learning techniques. A dataset of over 1,250 player performances from online matches was analyzed to identify underlying patterns in gameplay behavior. Unsupervised techniques, including t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) and Local Outlier Factor (LOF), were applied to uncover structural separation and abnormal player behavior within rank groups. Building on these insights, an XGBoost (XGBClassifier) model was developed to distinguish between smurf and non-smurf players using performance-based features. The modeling demonstrated strong detection capability, though some smurf cases exhibited behavior that was not clearly distinguishable from typical players, limiting classification performance. Overall, the findings suggest that behavioral features do provide meaningful support for smurf detection, while also highlighting inherent limitations driven by variability in player behavior.

Literature Review

Rank manipulation in the form of smurfing and boosting undermines competitive integrity by inserting high-skill performance into low-skill matchmaking, producing one-sided games and degrading player experience. Smurfing violates the fairness of what is assumed to be skill-based matchmaking environment and disrupts the natural power chain of rank progression or regression. Smurf detection is especially challenging early in an account’s life because traditional systems rely heavily on outcome-based understanding that require a sufficient number of matches to stabilize identification.

Outcome-based Rating Systems

Modern day rating systems primarily focus on the outcomes of matches or games, relative to typical performance patterns seen in different rating tiers. A large issue with these traditional systems is their inability to detect smurfing in the early-stages of an account. Using machine learning advantages, Shimanuki and Hosobe (2024)3 looked to create a rating system influenced by a fluctuating rating value defined as an Elo Factor, which uses features from game records in order to create a predictive ranking that is then compared to other players to find an expected outcome and finds either enhanced or delayed rating convergence based on clear player superiority.

Their work showcases how a much needed-enhancement of modern ranking systems can be created as a solution that manages to take out the aspect of smurfing that truly determines skill rather than consistent rising of ranks. However, their approach primarily focuses on adversarial board games and is limited in behavioral statistics available to those games. While useful for the setting of a board game, their research may not be as applicable to many online competitive games, but does show how an updated ranking system may play a factor in targeting smurfing.

Unsupervised Profiling and Smurf Identification

The most unethical part of smurfing is the manipulative and negative effects it has on the balancing of the rank-based competitive environment. Smurf players often have the performance-ability of higher ranked play, but choose to abuse it in lower ranked lobbies. Ding et al. (2024)5 aimed to create a modeling pipeline that utilized unsupervised techniques, such as PCA and K-means clustering, to identify irregular performance given the tier of a rank. While their approach proved to be high performing in its identified profiles, the performance was determined by a voter-based system from domain experts. In addition to this, the modeling techniques were limited to its unsupervised nature, which caused its reliance on existing domain experts. The research in this study hopes to combine unsupervised techniques with thorough, predictive analysis.

Behavioral Pattern Mining

The game Rocket League offers a distinctive opportunity in analyzing mechanical and behavioral patterns within the player population. Mathonat et al. (2020)2 uses this specific data availability to be able to create an interpretable model that detects user skill shot ability. Using the match-replay system, external controller information, and expert annotation, they were able to mine descriptive behavioral patterns within sequences of player action. Using these mined patterns, re-encoded sequences were created for supervised skill classification. Their analytical analysis is limited to the concept of identifying ability through skill shot behavior, but can be used for much more applicability, such as understanding smurfing behavior among different ranks.

Smurfing Impact on Experience

An important aspect of online competitive multiplayer games is that the players engage in a consumer-product relationship. They play these games and participate in their online communities, which provides value to the ecosystem of a game. To gather field research of the average CS:GO player, McCauley, B. (2023)8 over the span of about a year from March 2020 to June 2021 would play the competitive shooter CS:GO for over 1,600 hours to raise their rank to Nova 2. Following this process, they conducted interviews with a total of eight CS:GO players from March to June 2022, many of whom had smurf accounts or were on a similar rank to them, and through those interviews discussed their findings.

The first finding was that smurfing was embedded in the prosumer experience. It tends to have an impact on players, especially those in competitive fields, since they tend to unfairly cause drops in rankings. The second discovery from the interviews was the complex motivations of smurfs. Smurfs are not always malicious in intent and sometimes associate smurfing with positive outcomes for themselves by playing at lower levels for personal enjoyment due to high stress of rank upkeep. Lastly, McCauley, B. (2023)8 discusses whether the act of smurfing is generally seen as cheating. They concluded an indirect answer to the question, basing the opinion on perspective, and acknowledging that it can be seen as a lesser form of cheating compared to more-excessive forms such as hacking.

User Perception

Smurfing is widely recognized as a persistent issue within the online gaming sphere, negatively affecting player experience and perceptions of fairness. Monge and Matthews (2023)1 conducted a multi-study investigation at The Ohio State University examining player attitudes toward smurfing, beginning with a baseline survey of 328 participants recruited from gaming Subreddits and a university gaming club. Their results showed that 69% of respondents admitted to smurfing occasionally, albeit typically believing most smurfs are more hostile than themselves, and 94% believed that smurfing occurs regularly in the games they play. These findings indicate broad awareness of the issue as a common occurrence in a lot of online gaming spaces, and highlight the necessity of more matchmaking integrity.

Industry Landscape

Industry stakeholders have begun addressing this need through behavior-based analytics and account monitoring systems. TrustCloud (2023)4 categorizes behavioral pattern analysis as a core approach to countering smurf detection, by categorizing methods into behavior-based indicators (e.g. rapid performance progression), account-based signals, and identity verification. The report cites a 2022 North American machine learning experiment that reduced smurfing activity by over 30% by detecting deviations from expected player behavior profiles, demonstrating the commercial viability of behavioral detection methods. While some industry proposals emphasize stronger digital identity verification, these approaches raise concerns around privacy and accessibility, leaving behavior-driven detection as a scalable and widely applicable solution. TrustCloud (2023)4 primarily addresses the problem at the point of account creation, while this study looks to address it after the account is created.

Economic Impact of Player Churn

In the free to play video game ecosystem, revenue is linked to player retention. Research by Kang, H., Suh, C., & Kim, H. K. (2024)9 into competitive game logs found that matchmaking quality is a primary determinant of user churn. Their study demonstrated extreme skill gaps do not just cause individual match dissatisfaction, but correlate directly with a players decision to stop playing a game altogether. This “churn” is a financial risk, as Mulla et al. (2025)10 note, the cost of acquiring a new player via marketing is significantly higher than the cost of retaining a current one. By identifying smurf driven skill disparities early, developers can protect the long term sustainability of the game by appeasing current player satisfaction.

Detection Gaps

Traditional matchmaking systems, such as Elo, face limitations in rating convergence speed. Shimanuki and Hosobe (2024)3 found that outcomes based models that rely on just wins and losses are slow to stabilize an account's true skill level, particularly in new accounts. This delay in rating accuracy during the early stages of an accounts lifecycle creates a window where smurf accounts can disrupt dozens of matches before the matchmaking algorithm adjusts to their actual skill level. As the market for anti-cheat and fair play software is projected to reach $23.8 billion by 2032 (Verified Market Research, 2026)11, there is growing commercial and academic interest in proactive behavioral modeling. Systems that can infer skill through gameplay patterns rather than just win/loss records represent the next step in accelerating rating accuracy and competitive integrity.

Community

While peer-reviewed literature establishes the theoretical framework for player upset, qualitative data from community hubs like Reddit provide essential insight into the current user experience of the player base. These forums offer a real-time record of player sentiment that formal studies often lack. For instance, recent threads indicate that smurfing has transitioned from a minor nuisance to a primary driver of player upset. One newer player summarized their experience as "session fatigue" after encountering three smurfs in a single night, they said “I have played many competitive games and have not seen this consistent of smurfs before... I had to get off” (Children_Of_4, 2025). All over Rocket Leagues forums is the discussion of smurfing. Poster Yappaholic (2024)12 says “I’m so tired of smurfs,” describing a brand-new account that quickly climbed to high ranks in team play, performed advanced aerial maneuvers to dominate early play start, then quit early at 3:30.

Beyond formal studies, Lawler (2024)11, a popular Rocket League content creator and YouTuber, shows that creator commentary has started treating smurfing as a structural design problem in Rocket League’s ranked ecosystem, rather than just the behavior of some players. In his breakdown video made for the community analyzing Rocket League’s official changes, Lawler frames the issue as rooted in placement match design and MMR convergence speed. These systemic flaws let high skill players dominate low rank lobbies before their ratings are adjusted (Lawler, 2024)11. This creator-level analysis complements raw player frustration and highlights the need for improvement of current balancing systems.

By hearing community voices from both common players and influencers, it illustrates that both groups are explicitly calling for better smurf detection. These first-hand accounts from Reddit boards to Steam retention concerns reinforce a need for early behavioral profiling and modeling analysis systems.

Conclusion

These works suggest a realistic path toward early-stage smurf identification through the use of behavioral understanding via various machine-learning techniques. Previous works have shown the inclusion of mathematical influence to weigh early player performance, unsupervised techniques to identify irregularities within specific ranks, and how to identify behavioral patterns within non-outcome-based data. By combining the aspects from these different approaches, this research paper aims to create an early-stage system that can identify the likelihood of an account exhibiting smurf-like behavior, through the use of unsupervised and network-based modeling techniques.

2. Methodology

Data Collection

To build a behavioral dataset we scraped the website Ballchasing.com’s replay API, a community replay repository that hosts millions of parsed Rocket League replays with detailed per-player statistics. Using python, a curated scraping file was created that operates in two modes. The first, targets rank-isolated replay pools by querying the API with min-rank and max-rank filters set to the same rank tier. This ensures that every replay collected reflects genuine matchmaking at that specific rank. The second mode targets a curated group of suspected smurf replays by searching through a specific Ballchasing-filtered URL. This search then pulls all replays associated with those flagged accounts.

For each rank tier, across 20 different levels spanning from Bronze through Supersonic Legend, the scraping file fetched a target number of replay IDs. Using paginated API requests, downloaded was the pre-parsed player statistics converted into a CSV, for each replay group directly from Ballchasing’s stats export endpoint:
https://ballchasing.com/dl/stats/players/{replayID}/{replayID}-players.csv

Each individual CSV contains one row for each player per game, capturing over 70 behavioral statistics including performance data such as game scores, boost management, movement calculations, field positioning, and aerial ability. These files were saved directly to an online repository, contained in a structured folder hierarchy organized by rank tier, and simultaneously merged into a unified master_repays.csv. The scraping process included retry logic for rate limiting, HTML error page detection to catch failed downloads, and duplicate/skipping to ensure that previously downloaded replays were never re-fetched.

The master CSV required several cleaning processes before it could be used for analysis. The primary issue was a mixed-delimiter format introduced during the merge process, where the first four metadata columns (replay_id, playlist, rank, color) were stored as a single comma-separated string rather than individual columns. A dedicated script-fixer file was written to split and reformat each replay to produce a clean, fully comma-delimited file for data frame conversion.

Smurf labeling was performed manually because of the importing of replays from the specific Ballchasing group “potential-smurfs-enaka01dhi”, a community-reported collection of suspected smurf accounts. These replays were downloaded separately and merged into the master dataset. Players from this group were assigned the binary value of 1, while all rank-isolated replays collected through normal matchmaking were labeled with a value of 0. The final dataset contained 1,205 normal players and 51 labeled smurf records across various rank tiers.

Dataset Visualization

With the labeled data in hand, separation metrics were calculated for every available stat, including z-scores and the percentage of smurf players exceeding the normal player 90th percentile, in order to rank which behavioral signals most-reliably distinguish smurfs from legitimate players.

The most direct evidence of smurfing appears in Graph 1 (Point Disparity), which displays what assigned rank’s average score should be. In each rank a smurf was found, the smurf averaged consistently between +400 to as high as +1200 points above the expected score per game value. This ties in directly with Graph 2 (AVG Score), which breaks this down across each rank tier where smurfs were detected. Rather than having a single aggregate comparison, the chart displays the disparity in a way that make it impossible to ignore. At almost every rank from Bronze through Champion, the smurf bar dwarfs the normal player bar, with the most extreme case being at Platinum 3 where the smurfs averaged over 1,600 points against a normal average just below 500. One thing to note, smurfs were concentrated in the lower and middle tiers (Bronze through platinum) exactly where a skilled player would do the most damage before the matchmaking system catches up.

Scoring behavior is further broken down in Graph 3 (Top 5 Thresholds), which ranks the five strongest smurf detection statistics (score, goals, shots, big boost pads collected, and time in offensive half) by the percentage of smurfs that exceed the normal player 90th percentile. Score and goals were the strongest statistical signals, with over 90% of smurfs surpassing what a legitimate player achieves at the top of their rank.

Another indicator of a smurf is their movement and mechanical skill which were captured in Graph 4 (Speed Profile), which compares the proportion of game-time spent at slow, boost and supersonic speed. Along with that, Graph 5 (Speed Distribution) shows that the smurf speed curve is shifted noticeably rightward from normal players. Graph 6 (Air vs Ground) reinforces this, revealing that smurfs spend significantly more time in the air – a mechanical skill that takes hundreds of hours to develop and is nearly impossible to fake – compared to their rank placements. Another high level skill is displayed in graph 7 (Boost Management) and 8 (Boost Usage), which shows that smurfs collect/steal more boost overall and also maintain that boost to allow for those high-flying mechanics to take form. In graph 9 (Boost stealing), it highlights that smurfs are able to deny big boost pads at nearly 2.5 times the rate of normal players which is a high-skill habit that beginners do not recognize.

Positional tendencies are covered in Graph 10 (Pitch Positioning), showing that smurfs tend to spend more time in the offensive third allowing them to maintain pressure and score more goals. On the other hand, Graph 11 (Defensive Stats) reveals that smurfs concede dramatically fewer goals, which is most likely in relation to the amount of offensive pressure they produce. With over 90% of smurfs holding opponents to fewer than 2 goals per game compared to only 25% of normal players. Finally, Graphs 12 and 13 (Heatmaps) reveal the structural differences in how stats relate to each other between the two groups, suggesting that smurfs operate with a fundamentally different and more coordinated play style than a normal player at lower ranks would have.

Dimensionality Reduction

The first step of the modeling pipeline was to give evidence of behavioral distinction between not just the players within the dataset, but between their respective ranks. This meant showcasing any potential playstyle or philosophical differences that influenced different skill groups amongst each rank. By creating distinct behavioral profiles, evidence of abnormal deviation from skill groups could then be calculated. Given the complexity and size of the player data, unsupervised dimensionality reduction was performed.

Opposed to other unsupervised dimensionality reduction techniques, t-SNE was utilized for its non-linear component analysis that paired with the complex nature of the data. The purely-numeric columns of the data were standardized and fit into a t-SNE run of different values of the perplexity parameter. Perplexity controls the balance between local and global structure in t-SNE by determining the effective number of neighbors considered when constructing the embedding. After visual validation of different values, a perplexity value of 20 was deemed fit for a final run of dimensionality reduction. It should be noted that after thorough internal discussion, it was chosen that t-SNE was to be on all players, instead of running separate versions of t-SNE for each skill group.

In graphs 14 and 15, the newly constructed dimension space of the dataset can be seen in two-dimensional and three-dimensional plots. From an initial glance and the coloring of data points, there is visual evidence of distinctive separation within ranks. To create additional, mathematical evidence of this, the t-SNE coordinates were used in two cases of interest that were added into predicting smurf likelihood.

k-Means clustering was ran on the t-SNE coordinates, with a cross-validated clustering value of 5. Graph 16 visualizes this clustering behavior accordingly. These clustering assignments were then added to the dataset as an additional predictor, in case of any potential influence in smurf identification. The second case of interest, more fit to the predictive scenario of the research, was determining outliers within the dimension space. Local Outlier Factoring, known as LOF, was ran to calculate density-based neighboring scores of each data point. These calculations were then assigned to each player to keep track of later in the pipeline. In graph 15, the coloring of each data point matched their respective LOF score and what can be seen are several suspicious players whose color is significantly different from the majority. These players likely had the most negative calculations based on the LOF scaling and influenced their visual flagging. As mentioned, these LOF calculations were then assigned respectively and later used in the prediction of smurf likelihood.

Smurf Prediction

Now that there is both visual and mathematical evidence of behavioral profiles within the playerbase, this unsupervised insight can be used in identifying potential smurf behavior. Given the dataset had manually inputted smurf labeling, a classification model can be created. Given the high dimensionality and nonlinear relationships present within the dataset, an XGBoost Classifier model was selected due to its ability to capture these complex feature interactions, handle feature scaling, and perform robustly on this heavily-imbalanced classification scenario.

An initial run of the XGBClassifier was ran to give a first glimpse of performance and a scope for optimization later. The data is split into train and test splits, while default parameters are used on the model. Results of this run can be seen in table 2. From the results, it can be concluded that while the model can run moderately with default parameterization, the goal of the research is to create high performing smurf identifications. In addition to this, because of the imbalanced ratio of smurfs to non-smurfs, it needs to be ensured that the model can handle a much larger dataset seen in an actual playerbase. Additionally, a threshold value for prediction should also be optimized to ensure that it is relative to the scope of the model’s understanding of the data. After testing different threshold values, a predictive threshold value of 0.13 was chosen. Although the threshold seems low in a general modeling ideology, since the scenario requires high-performance anomaly detection, the imbalance of the prediction class, and the testing of prediction probabilities, this threshold was deemed acceptable relative to the scenario. Threshold performance can be seen in table 3.

To accomplish modeling improvement, Randomized Grid Search cross-validation was used on a Stratified k-Fold splitting. Stratified k-Fold splits the data into data groupings that respect the imbalanced distribution of the smurf class. Utilizing these cultivated data splits, Randomized Grid Search then went through random combinations of the model’s parameters to efficiently find an optimized set of parameters that can be used in a tuned run of the model. After the hyper-tuned parameters for the XGBClassifier was found, a final run of prediction was ran using the newly optimized model.

Results

From the unsupervised dimensionality reduction, clear patterns of behavioral structure emerged within the dataset. The t-SNE embeddings revealed visual separation across ranks, suggesting that gameplay statistics capture meaningful differences in player behavior, such as playstyle and performance tendencies. In addition to these visual patterns, Local Outlier Factor (LOF) analysis provided further quantitative support for these distinctions. The resulting outlier calculations indicated that certain players exhibited statistically atypical behavior, relative to their peers within the same rank. These calculated outlier scores further reinforced the presence of identifiable behavioral variation within the playerbase.

For the smurf class, the predictive model achieved a precision of 0.64 and a recall of 0.70, resulting in an F1-score of 0.67. This indicates that the model is able to identify the majority of smurf players at a moderate-to-high performance rate. Specifically, approximately 70% of smurfs are correctly identified, though about 36% of players flagged as smurfs are actually non-smurfs who truly play at a high skill-level. These results can be found in Table 4. It should also be noted that both before optimization and after optimization, three true-smurfs were consistently unable to be flagged by the model. Further investigation into these three cases was performed, manually re-evaluating their respective game performances. It was later determined that their in-game performance metrics did not strongly differentiate from typical players, suggesting that some smurfs may not exhibit clear behavioral deviations within a single match. This is expanded on in the Discussion section. Overall, the model demonstrates moderate-to-high effectiveness in distinguishing smurf behavior, while also highlighting inherent limitations in detecting players whose performance does not strongly deviate from the norm.

Discussion

The analytical pipeline utilized in the study illustrated both the significance of smurfing in the gaming industry and the difficulty present in addressing it conclusively. The goal of smurfs is to deceive existing competitive environments and hinder the skill-based matchmaking that aims to balance competitive fairness. If left unchecked, players who are true to their determined rankings are made to play in online matches that run rampant of players more-skilled than them. Justifiably, players will grow more and more exhausted with this experience, eventually influencing the decline of a game’s player base. While the techniques and algorithms employed in this research aim to address this issue from a statistical standpoint, they still remain limited in their scope of a larger moral and security issue at hand.

When reflecting over the main question of this research, it can still be concluded that the combination of both unsupervised dimensionality reduction and predictive analysis are effective in identifying smurf-behaving players. t-SNE component reduction allowed for the data to be transformed into a component space that visualized behavioral separation. This allowed for the calculation of clustering groups and outlier scores that further influenced smurf likelihood. By optimizing a highly-complex classifier model, that took into account the in-game performance of players along with the insights of the unsupervised techniques, a smurf detection system was cultivated. By training this system on an understanding of player-behavior, it was able to detect the majority of true anomalies, despite the heavy imbalance of smurfs to non-smurfs, within the player pool.

Notable Findings

From the predictive analysis, additional modeling insights were pulled that expanded on the pipeline’s understanding of distinction between smurfs and non-smurfs. The top 9 most-influential statistics that determined a smurf included: score, shots, saves, goals, shots conceded, avg speed, count powerslide, percentage high in air, avg distance to ball no possession, as can be seen in table 5. This excludes LOF scoring, which also revealed high influence. The mean averages of these statistics for each prediction class are seen in table 6. From this table, the average gap between a non-smurf and a true smurf can be seen in these statistics, giving more weight to the issue of competitive integrity. Discussing about the nature of these categories specifically, while score, shots, saves, and goals act more as results-based indicators, stats like avg speed, count powerslide, and percentage high in air, act as the behavioral-based indicators that this study wanted to exclaim.

Limitations

The performance metrics that resulted from this analysis revealed that while the majority of predictions can be correctly captured at a high-performance rate, that same behavioral nature of the data hindered the understanding of this detection system. The pipeline was able to process, interpret, and understand the distinction between a regular player and a statistical anomaly within a skill group. However, it was unable to detect a player who was not an obvious outlier or edge-case statistically. In other words, while most smurfs appeared as statistically superior in their gameplay, this modeling system was unable to detect the accounts who played more-naturally to their similarly-ranked peers. This can speak to an insight issue with the kind of data worked with in this study but can also speak to the morally deceptive aspect of this online issue. To solve this problem statistically, additional behavioral features could be included in replays to give more potential insight to predictive analysis.
